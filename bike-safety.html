These rules are written in simple language. You can read them to children, or use them to explain why safety matters.

1. Always wear your helmet

A helmet is a strong safety hat. If the bike slips or something hits you, it protects your brain. Think of it as a superhero shield for your head.

2. The person behind you also needs a helmet

The back seat is not safe without a helmet. If the bike stops suddenly, the person behind can fall. Both rider and pillion should wear helmets every time.

3. Never take three people on a bike

A bike is made for two people only. With three, the bike becomes shaky and hard to balance ‚Äî just like a small chair that breaks when too many people sit on it.

4. Use indicators whenever you turn

The blinking orange lights tell others which way you want to go. If you don‚Äôt use them, drivers behind you may guess wrong and hit your bike.

5. Never ride on the wrong side of the road

On the wrong side, vehicles come fast towards you. They do not expect a bike there. One mistake can cause a head-on crash, which is very dangerous.

6. Avoid loud or modified exhausts

Very loud bikes disturb people, hospitals and animals. Many cities also fine such bikes. A safe bike is one that sounds normal, not like a rocket.

7. No mobile phone while riding

If you look at your phone, your eyes are not on the road and your hands are not fully on the handle. It‚Äôs like trying to read a book while running ‚Äî you will hit something.

8. Keep space from other vehicles

Always keep a gap from the vehicle in front. If it stops suddenly, you get time to brake. When you are too close, even a small mistake can cause a crash.

9. Ride in a straight line, not zig-zag

Rapid lane-changing and zig-zag riding confuse other drivers. Ride smooth and straight, the way you draw a neat line in your notebook.

10. Wear proper shoes, not slippers

Slippers can slip off or get stuck in the bike. Closed shoes give better grip on the brake and protect your feet ‚Äî like seatbelts for your legs.

Tip for parents: pick one rule each day and talk about it with your child. Repeating simple messages builds strong safety habits.
